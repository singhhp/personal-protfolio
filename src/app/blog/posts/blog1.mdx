## Introduction

One of the most overlooked topics among data science enthusiasts is **statistics**.  
Many individuals eager to explore and learn data science tend to either ignore or skip this foundational concept. However, statistics is the **backbone of data analysis**, enabling us to extract meaningful insights from raw data.

This article serves as an introduction to some fundamental statistical concepts that are essential to understand before diving into data science.

---

## Broad Classification of Statistics

Statistics can be broadly classified into two main categories:

1. **Descriptive Statistics**  
2. **Inferential Statistics**

---

## Descriptive Statistics

Descriptive statistics involve summarizing data to provide a clear and concise representation of its characteristics. It includes measures such as:

- **Central Tendency**: Mean, Median, Mode  
- **Dispersion**: Range, Variance, Standard Deviation  
- **Shape of Distribution**: Skewness and Kurtosis  

The primary goal of descriptive statistics is to highlight key features of the data, providing insights into what the data is communicating.  
Additionally, data visualization techniques such as **histograms, bar graphs, pie charts, and scatter plots** help in understanding patterns within the dataset.

---

## Understanding Key Concepts of Descriptive Statistics

### Measure of Central Tendency

#### Mean
Mathematically, the mean is obtained by dividing the sum of all observations by the total number of observations.  
It is often referred to as the **average**.

**Example:**  
Marks of students in a class:  
`80, 75, 65, 85, 92, 85, 92, 32, 78, 94, 96, 89, 78, 93, 78, 87`

---

#### Median
The **median** is the middle value of an ordered dataset.  
If the number of observations is odd, it’s the exact middle value.  
If even, it’s the average of the two middle values.

**Example:**  
Sorted data:  
`32, 65, 75, 78, 78, 78, 80, 85, 85, 87, 89, 92, 92, 93, 94, 96`

Since there are 16 numbers, the median is the **average of the 8th and 9th values**.

---

#### Mode
The **mode** is the value that appears most frequently in a dataset.

**Example:**  
In the given dataset, 85 appears more frequently than any other number.  
Hence, the mode is **85**.

---

### Why Are All Three Measures Important?

Let’s analyze two datasets:

**Dataset 1:** `2, 3, 6, 5, 7, 8, 9`  
- Mean = 5.7  
- Median = 6  

**Dataset 2:** `2, 3, 6, 5, 7, 8, 9, 100, 102, 105`  
- Mean = 34.7  
- Median = 7.5  

Notice how the **mean changes drastically** when high outliers are added, whereas the **median remains stable**.  
This demonstrates the **impact of outliers**, which can distort statistical measures.  
Hence, it’s essential to consider all measures of central tendency for accurate insights.

## Measures of Variability

Variability describes the **spread of data** — how far individual values deviate from the mean.  
Three common measures include:

### Range
The **range** is the difference between the highest and lowest values.

**Example:**  
Marks: `80, 75, 65, 85, 92, 85, 92, 32, 78, 94, 96, 89, 78, 93, 78, 87`  
Range = 96 - 32 = **64**

---

### Standard Deviation
The **standard deviation (SD)** measures the average deviation of each value from the mean.

**Example:**  
Dataset: `2, 8, 7, 6, 9, 10`  
Mean = 7  
Sum of squared deviations = 40  
n = 6  

\[
SD = \sqrt{(40 / 6)} = 2.58
\]

Thus, the average deviation from the mean is **2.58**.

---

### Variance
Variance represents the degree of spread and is the **square of the standard deviation**.

\[
Variance = (2.58)^2 = 6.67
\]
